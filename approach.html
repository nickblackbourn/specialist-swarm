Building an APMP-Grade AI Bid Desk

How we turned a working multi-agent prototype into a system that writes bid responses the way a qualified APMP capture lead would — and why the first version, despite being fluent and well-structured, was always going to lose the deal.

Stack: Claude Managed Agents · Custom Skills API Build time: ~60 min v1 · +3 hrs APMP uplift Scenario: Acme Corp RFP (synthetic)

Headline

  1. The naive build worked. A coordinator agent plus four specialist sub-agents produced a complete, branded Word document in under two minutes. The pipeline was sound.
  2. The output was undifferentiated. Every claim was something a competitor could also make. The bid was a fact-dump, not an argument — and would not have won.
  3. The fix was structural, not editorial. We layered APMP bid-management discipline into the skills the agents consume. Same architecture, same agents — different methodology, materially different proposal.

01The brief

A 60-minute workshop build, modelling how a senior services firm actually runs a deal.

Services firms have a structural problem: too many inbound RFPs, too few hours of senior partner time to read them properly. The naive answer — “point an LLM at the RFP and ask for a proposal” — produces a confident-sounding document that loses to better-prepared competitors.

Our brief was to build the architecture that does map to senior practice: a coordinator who orchestrates, specialists who own lanes, and a final deliverable that survives serious scrutiny. The scenario was a synthetic Acme Corp RFP for an enterprise data platform — a deal with real curveballs: a 35% minimum-discount demand, uncapped breach liability, full IP assignment, and a non-negotiable Power BI integration.

Four specialists run in parallel. Each consults its own domain skill. The coordinator (now also carrying a win-strategy skill) sequences the work and assembles the final document.

The pattern is deliberately mundane: it is exactly how a partner-led deal desk runs offline. A senior orchestrator reads the brief, dispatches to four specialists in parallel, synthesises their work, and signs the deliverable. The whole point of the architecture is that it makes the AI behave the way the human practice already does — including the parts of that practice that produce winning documents rather than merely complete ones.

03Run 1 — a document, but not a winning one

The first build worked technically. Then we read what it produced.

With everything wired up, we sent the Acme RFP through the swarm. The events stream behaved exactly as designed: four parallel session.thread_created events, four specialists working concurrently, four replies returning to the coordinator, a python-docx call to build the file, a downloaded BTS-Synthetic_Proposal_Acme_Corp.docx in the outputs folder. Total elapsed time: under two minutes.

Then we opened the document. It was complete. It was well-structured. It would have lost the deal.

Specifically, every section was a fact-dump: the pricing section recited discount bands; the legal section listed counter-positions in deal-desk voice (“BLOCKER”, “we will not accept”); the technical section paraded capabilities; the competitive section produced battlecards. There was no win strategy threading the document, no persona-tailored executive summary, no ghosting, no customer-language discipline, and no specific call to action. It read like a vendor talking about itself.

The capability matrix is not the proposal. The win themes are. — APMP first principle

04The diagnosis

AI inherits the discipline you put in front of it. We had given it none.

It would have been easy to blame the model. The actual root cause was structural: the skills we had handed the specialists were internal artifacts — pricing rule-tables, legal blocker-lists, competitor battlecards. They were excellent at producing a deal-desk position. They had nothing to say about how to write a customer-facing bid.

The coordinator’s system prompt told it to “synthesise the specialist outputs into a proposal”. Without an explicit win-strategy step, the coordinator did exactly that — assembled the specialist outputs end-to-end, in their internal voices, with no recurring argument. The output was the sum of the inputs. Generic in, generic out.

Once we framed the problem that way, the fix was obvious. We didn’t need new agents, new tools, or new orchestration. We needed the skills layer to carry the bid-management methodology that a qualified APMP capture lead carries in their head.

05The APMP uplift

Five skills, two prompt rewrites, one orchestration change. Same agents, same architecture.

The uplift had three layers:

Rewrote the three existing skills to APMP standard

pricing-playbook Rewritten

Kept the internal rule-tables. Added Price-to-Win discipline, anchor-on-TCO framing, value-anchor library, give/get choreography, BAFO posture, and an explicit play for refusing the 35% discount demand without saying no. Now produces both a deal-desk position and a customer-facing commercial narrative.

legal-checklist Rewritten

Kept the redline checklist. Added a customer-facing risk-reframe library: every BLOCKER paired with the language that frames our counter-position as Acme being better-protected than they drafted. Doctrine: we sell peace of mind, not legalese.

competitive-intel Rewritten

Kept the battlecards. Added the discriminator framework (customer-cares × we-have × they-don’t), a ghosting voice library (six patterns), win-theme alignment, and a reference-deployment guide (which past win supports which message).

Added two new skills

win-strategy New · coordinator

The APMP heart. Hot-button analysis, persona mapping, win-theme construction (max five, each = hot button × discriminator × proof), message map, persona-tailored executive summary template, action-caption discipline, ghosting voice, and a 10-question colour-team quality gate before the document is finalised.

solution-architect New · technical fit

The Technical Fit specialist previously had no skill at all. Now it has one teaching capability-to-outcome translation, the NPI doctrine for honest gap handling (name → reframe → mitigate → prove), architecture-as-story narrative, phased implementation patterns, and customer-facing risk registers.

Rewired the prompts

The coordinator’s system prompt was rewritten to invoke the win-strategy skill before any specialist delegation, to brief the specialists with the win themes (not just the RFP), and to synthesise against the message map rather than the raw specialist outputs. Each specialist’s prompt was rewritten to require two outputs: an internal artifact in their usual format, and a customer-facing narrative block aligned to the assigned win themes.

06The bid director’s sequence

What the coordinator now does, every time, before producing a document.

  1. Hot-button & hidden-need analysis. What the customer explicitly says they need, and what the RFP reveals between the lines (career protection, procurement defensibility, fear of past pain).
  2. Persona map. Who reads each section. What they need to defend. What they fear. What tone they respond to.
  3. Win themes. Three to five. Each constructed as hot button × discriminator × proof. Each must survive the “so what?” and “who else?” tests.
  4. Message map. Which theme owns which section. Discriminators are seeded across sections, not siloed.
  5. Specialist briefs. Each specialist receives the win themes, the persona map, and a specific output spec — not just the RFP.
  6. Synthesis to the map. The proposal is built against the message map, not stitched from raw specialist outputs.
  7. Colour-team quality gate. Ten questions answered “yes” before the document is finalised: win themes stateable in one sentence, ghosting present, NPI handled, “you” leads paragraphs, action captions on visuals, specific call to action.
  8. Document. Branded Word file in the required structure.

The win-theme construction is the single most leveraged step. Every theme is written to a formula the coordinator can verify itself against: 

# Win theme construction formula
WIN THEME = HOT BUTTON  ×  DISCRIMINATOR  ×  PROOF
            (customer-care)  (we-do, they-don’t)  (named evidence)

# Worked example, generated on the Acme run:
“Because Acme has 600 Power BI users who cannot be retrained, and a
 2027 deadline to retire Teradata, Acme needs an analytics layer that
 drops underneath the existing Power BI estate without a forklift.
 Our dedicated Power BI DirectQuery adapter — the same architecture
 Initech Sensors chose over Microsoft Fabric in 2025 — delivers that
 without locking Acme back into a single vendor.”

Notice the three legs: a hot button quoted in the customer’s own language (“600 Power BI users”, “2027”), a discriminator specific enough to be defensible (“dedicated DirectQuery adapter”, not “great BI”), and a named proof point (“Initech Sensors”, not “a Fortune 500 customer”). Drop any leg and the theme collapses to a marketing line.

07Run 2 — APMP-grade output

Same RFP, same agents, same architecture. Materially different document.

With the new skills in place, we re-ran the swarm. The coordinator’s first action was no longer to delegate. It was to produce a win-strategy brief — hot buttons with RFP-section citations, hidden needs surfaced, persona map, five themes, message map, NPI items flagged — before any specialist was contacted. Then it briefed each specialist with the brief, in parallel.

During synthesis, the coordinator also did real editorial work: when the Competitive Intel specialist overclaimed sub-100ms latency in its draft, the coordinator caught it and rewrote that section to preserve the NPI integrity of the gap-disclosure elsewhere in the bid. Before producing the document, the coordinator ran itself through the 10-question colour-team gate and logged the pass.

Both documents are kept side-by-side in the project’s outputs/ folder. The size delta isn’t the point — a longer proposal isn’t a better one. The point is that every paragraph in Run 2 carries an argument that one of the five themes makes; in Run 1, paragraphs carried facts.

08The general pattern

This isn’t just for bid responses.

The build pattern generalises to any structured services-firm output where a senior orchestrator needs to assemble specialist contributions into a coherent deliverable. The interesting ones:

  • M&A diligence memos. A coordinator routing to financial, legal, tech-stack and people-culture specialists, with a methodology skill that drives the risk-and-recommendation discipline.
  • Compliance and regulatory reviews. Coordinator + control-framework specialists + a methodology skill that drives evidence-citation discipline.
  • Strategic onboarding plans. Coordinator + functional specialists + a methodology skill that drives day-1-readiness discipline.
  • Technical due diligence. Coordinator + architecture / security / code-quality / ops specialists + a methodology skill that drives the buyer’s investment thesis.

In each case the constant is the same: a structured methodology in the skills layer, parallel specialists, and a senior-practice orchestrator that knows the trade and the customer.

Generic AI loses bids. Structured AI wins them. The skills layer is where firm-specific expertise lives — defensible, reusable, evolvable.

09What we’d carry forward

The non-obvious lessons from the build.

The skills layer is the leverage point. The agents, the orchestration, the model choice — all of these matter at the margin. The skills are where the firm’s methodology lives. Investing carefully there pays back across every deal the system handles.

Internal artifacts make terrible customer-facing prose. Every domain we touched had this problem. The deal-desk rule-tables, the legal blocker lists, the engineering capability matrices — all critical for internal alignment, all poison if they survive into a customer document. The skills now explicitly carry two layers: internal and customer-facing.

Coordinators must be bid directors, not just dispatchers. A coordinator that simply parallelises and synthesises produces a Frankenstein. A coordinator that runs a deliberate strategy step before delegating, and synthesises to a message map after, produces a coherent argument.

Quality gates earn their cost. The 10-question colour-team gate adds perhaps thirty seconds to the run. It catches the things that would have lost the deal: capability-dump executive summaries, “we”-walls, hidden gaps, missing calls to action.

10What’s next

The natural extensions, in priority order.

  • Critic sub-agent. A fifth agent that reviews the coordinator’s draft against the colour-team questions and returns objections before the document is finalised. Adds a structural Red-team review without slowing the path-of-least-resistance run.
  • Firm-voice skill. Every services firm has a recognisable house style. Codify it as a skill the coordinator carries, and apply it at synthesis time.
  • Memory across deals. The coordinator remembers past wins and re-uses the right ones — not just from past-wins.json but from real CRM data via an MCP server.
  • Synthetic MCP for past wins. Wire up a fake CRM (or a real one) and let the pricing specialist query it during the bid rather than reading a static file.
